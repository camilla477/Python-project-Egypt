📌 Key Findings

Arab Spring (2011-2013): A massive wave of public unrest driven by widespread dissatisfaction with President Mubarak. The first two months (January 25 - February 28, 2011) represented the peak of mass mobilization, which led to Mubarak's resignation on February 11, 2011. The movement continued with key peaks during important political moments, including the October 2011 parliamentary elections announcement and the July 2013 military coup.


2019-2020 Protests: A new wave of protests sparked against President Al-Sisi's authoritarian rule. Despite significantly lower frequency (a 93.74% drop compared to Arab Spring), these protests are considered the most active demonstrations after 2011. Key peaks occurred in September 2019 and September 2020, with September 2020 marking increased activism on the one-year anniversary.

⏰ Temporal Patterns

Arab Spring Peak: The first two months of 2011 (January 25 - February 28) saw the highest frequency of demonstrations. The momentum continued with notable peaks in October 2011 (140 activities, driven by parliamentary elections) and July 2013 (201 activities, the military coup). This demonstrates how major political events drove protest frequency.


2019-2020 Pattern: September 2019 experienced 19 protests that slowly declined due to state repression. In September 2020, on the one-year anniversary, Egyptians took to the streets again with 52 recorded demonstrations, only to be harshly repressed in late September, marking the end of this wave of mass mobilization.

🗺️ Spatial Shifts

Arab Spring Geography: Cairo dominated overwhelmingly with 1,177 demonstrations, followed by Giza (204) and Alexandria (171). Demonstrations were widespread and equally distributed across major urban centers and along the Nile River.


2019-2020 Shift: Cairo experienced a dramatic 98% drop (from 1,177 to 20 events), due to intense surveillance and heavy policing in the capital. Giza emerged as the new hub with 42 events, while previously less relevant governorates like Beheira (10) and Gharbia (8) gained prominence. This reflects a strategic shift to more decentralized, rural demonstrations to avoid state surveillance.

⚠️ Violence & Repression Trends

Arab Spring Character: Largely peaceful protests with massive mobilization emphasizing non-violent resistance. Violent demonstrations remained consistent (22-24% range), with only a minority of incidents showing excessive force by state police. State intervention in protests ranged from 4.5-5.1%.


2019-2020 Transformation: These protests were significantly more peaceful overall (nearly 80% in 2019 were peaceful), with violent demonstrations dropping to 4.3% (2019) and 8% (2020). However, state violence reached unprecedented levels:

  • State Intervention: Rose from 4.5-5.1% (Arab Spring) to 14.9% (2019) and 48% (2020)
  • Excessive Force: Significantly increased, reflecting systematic repression
  • Pattern: State response was violent regardless of protester behavior

This demonstrates a clear shift from a protest-driven conflict (Arab Spring) to a state-repression-dominated scenario (2019-2020), reflecting the authoritarian consolidation under President Al-Sisi.

🔍 Key Insights

1. Scale Reduction: Total protest events dropped 93.74% from 2,323 to 147, reflecting the effectiveness of authoritarian repression measures like Law 107/2013.


2. Geographic Decentralization: Power shifted from Cairo (98% drop in events) to peripheral governorates, suggesting strategic adaptation to avoid surveillance.


3. Nonviolence Increase: 2019-2020 protests were more peaceful than Arab Spring, yet faced far greater state repression—a paradox revealing systematic state suppression.


4. Repression as Strategy: State intervention increased from ~5% to ~48%, indicating that the authoritarian regime deployed systematic violence regardless of protester behavior.


5. Authoritarian Consolidation: The shift from protest-driven conflict to state-repression-dominated dynamics reflects Al-Sisi's consolidation of authoritarian control and the normalization of political crackdowns.
